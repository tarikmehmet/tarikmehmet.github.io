Federica Facchin1 and Davide Margola1

Abstract

This study identified the main components of the drugs and crime experience of a sample of 25 drug-dependent inmates interviewed in prison. Text analyses were conducted using a phenomenological method. The sample was characterized by a disruptive childhood in multi-problematic families and deviant social contexts where drug use and crime were considered normal since early adolescence. Drug initiation involved recreational use of dance drugs and/ or cocaine, and the pleasure experienced was identified as the cause of subsequent persistent use. Three pathways that led to dependence were identified: The narcissistic pathway was defined as involving uncontrolled cocaine and amphetamines to feel powerful and limitless. The posttraumatic pathway was defined as involving post-trauma self- destructive drug use, while the pain relief pathway was defined as involving multiple substances to relieve pain. The second and third pathways were more directly associated with crime aimed at sustaining drug use.

Keywords

addiction; Italy; lived experience; phenomenological method; prisons / prisoners

Introduction

Several models have demonstrated a link between drug use and crime (see reviews by Bennett, Holloway, & Farrington, 2008; Colman & Vandam, 2009; Gandossy, Williams, Cohen, & Harwood, 1980; Hough, 1996; see also Bowser, Word, & Seddon, 2014; Martin, Macdonalds,

&Ishiguro, 2013; Walters, 2014) and the importance of a comprehensive understanding of this link for social pol- icy (Hammersley, 2011; Seddon, 2000).

Although the link between drug use and crime can be described as part of a more complex interaction process that involves multiple factors operating at different levels (Seddon, 2000), in several studies, it has been conceptual- ized as the result of a linear cause–effect relationship (i.e., “drug-use-causes-crime” or “crime-causes-drug-use”) or as the result of a third variable like self-control—hence the label “common cause” (Bennett et al., 2008; Colman & Vandam, 2009; see also Walters, 2015). In this article, we present a qualitative study that identifies methodological and conceptual tools for the drug–crime connection. We begin by discussing evidence for the “drug-use-causes- crime,” “crime-causes-drug-use,” and “common cause” models before turning our attention to non-causal pathway explanation models that inspired the current research.

According to the tripartite model developed by Goldstein (1985), the nature of the drug–crime link can

be explained from a psychopharmacological, economic– compulsive, or a systemic perspective. The psychophar- macological perspective holds that drug use causes crime due to its biochemical effects on the individual, such as psychotic symptoms and mental health problems in gen- eral, mood disorders, irritability, aggressive and violent impulses, and therefore judgment impairment. The economic–compulsive perspective argues that expensive drug use causes crime because the user needs money to buy drugs, while the systemic perspective holds that crime, such as thefts, assaults, and violent crimes, are related to the illegal drug market (Bennett et al., 2008; Colman & Vandam, 2009; Seddon, 2000). The psycho- pharmacological as well as the economic–compulsive explanations are considered the most common “drug-use- causes-crime” theories (Bennett et al., 2008).

Other research provides evidence for the economic– compulsive theory, suggesting that substance users com- mit crimes, more frequently property crimes, but also drug dealing, prostitution, and begging, to fund their drug use (Ball, Rosen, Flueck, & Nurco, 1981; Bennett et al., 2008; Farabee, Joshi, & Anglin, 2001; Hutchinson, Gore, Taylor, Goldberg, & Frisher, 2000; H. Research support for the systemic theory of the drug–crime connection (Colman & Vandam, 2009) has found associations between the illegal local drug market and violence in inner cities (Inciardi, 1999).

In terms of social and health care policies, the “drug- use-causes-crime” model would imply, for example, that effective treatments for problematic drug use such as offering a free supply of drugs (e.g., heroin or methadone prescriptions) should minimize criminal behaviors (Seddon, 2000). Although this model is attractive because it seems logical and self-evident, research shows a more complex scenario.

Several studies have demonstrated that drug intoxi- cation does not consistently lead to aggressive and/or criminal behaviors (Fagan, 1990), while alcohol use (alone or combined with other drugs) seems to be more related to violent crimes rather than illicit substances (Fagan, 1990; Goldstein, Brownstein, Ryan, & Bellucci, 1989; R. Parker & Auerhahn, 1998). Other studies reveal that crime might precede drug use (Hall, 1996; Kaye, Darke, & Finlay-Jones, 1998) or increase it, so that the greater income from drug sales allows an offender to buy more drugs (Burr, 1987). Moreover, offenders can use drugs as a “chemical recreation” to celebrate a successful crime (Menard, Mihalic, & Huizinga, 2001). These findings can be summarized into a “crime-causes-drug-use” model (Bennett et al., 2008), according to which the link between drug use and crime is conceptualized as a single cause–effect expla- nation that implies linear causality.

The causal pathway also involves a third set of theo- ries, which assume that the drug–crime connection should not be explained in terms of dualistic linear causality, as these two variables are both caused by a third sociologi- cal, psychological, environmental, or situational variable, for example, low self-control (Gottfredson & Hirschi, 1990). This conceptualization of the drug–crime connec- tion is known as the “common cause” explanation (Bennett et al., 2008).

The Non-Causal Perspective

All three models described above imply the same “deter- ministic” idea of direct causality. The question of causal- ity in the research on the drug–crime link has been addressed by Seddon (2000) who asserted, “deterministic unidirectional cause-effect models are conceptually inad- equate for examining the drug-crime relationship” (p. 102). Seddon (2000) suggests that an adequate model should conceptualize the connection between drug use and crime as part of a more complex interaction process that involves multiple factors operating and interacting at different levels, moreover in a non-causal manner.

Only a few studies provide evidence for theories that can be grouped into a non-causal model (Hammersley, 2008, 2011; Simpson, 2003). These theories consider drug use and crime as associated but not causally (Bennett et al., 2008), because the drugs–crime relationship is influenced by many other factors. These factors include things such as socio-cultural variables or the existence of deviant subcultures that may encourage or even reward drug use and criminal behaviors as well as personality traits such as sensation seeking/extraversion and specific disorders (e.g., antisocial, borderline, and narcissistic personality disorders). Other factors include disruptive childhood and drug-related stress (e.g., nearly dying from overdose, or seeing a friend die from overdose), as well as mutual facilitation of substance use and crime (Neale, 2000). Heavy drug use and drug dependence, which may lead to criminal activities to sustain the economic costs of drug use, is another factor as well as the specific psycho- active effects of drugs, which may facilitate specific types of crimes. This perspective is comprehensive as it does not assume the primacy of one variable over others in determining criminal behaviors or drug use. In fact, drug users and the drug–crime link are too complex to be explained in causal terms because they depend on the co- occurrence and interaction of multiple factors (Simpson, 2003). As suggested by Seddon (2000), although most research investigating the drug–crime link is quantitative, qualitative research methods may be the best tool to investigate how and why this process works, as they allow more in-depth analyses.

The goal of the current qualitative study is to identify the main components (e.g., themes, subthemes, and meanings) of the lived experience of drugs and crime with drug-dependent inmates. What participants’ words say about themselves (Pennebaker, Facchin, & Margola, 2010; see also Facchin, Margola, Molgora, & Revenson, 2014; Margola, Facchin, Molgora, & Revenson, 2010), as well as the larger social discourse they are part of, was analyzed in the current research to gain a better under- standing of the drug–crime relationship.

Method

Participants and Procedure

This study investigated the lived drug–crime experience of dependent drug users in an Italian prison using a semi- structured interview. Purposive sampling was used to recruit a group of inmates diagnosed with drug depen- dence by the Public Service for Addictions operating inside the prison. The diagnosis was made according to the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (4th ed., text rev.; DSM-IV-TR; American Psychiatric Association [APA], 2000) criteria because the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (5th ed.; DSM-5; APA, 2013), Italian version (APA, 2014), was published after the present study was completed.

Twenty-five participants (26–68 years of age), fluent in Italian, representative of different age groups, levels of education, and types of crime were included in the present study. Participant education levels were 12% primary, 72% middle, and 16% high school. Cause of current incarcera- tion was 52% for drug crimes (e.g., drug dealing and drug possession), 36% for person crimes (e.g., murder, robbery, assault), and 12% for property crimes (e.g., burglary, theft, shoplifting). Drug use among participants was 92% cocaine, 4% heroin, and 4% methamphetamine. It should be noted that the high percentage of dependent cocaine users in our sample is not surprising, considering that cocaine is the second only to cannabis as the most widely used substance in Italy (see the European Monitoring Centre for Drugs and Drug Addiction [EMCDDA], 2014 annual report). Only male inmates were included in this study to increase sample homogeneity, as indicated in phe- nomenological research (Smith, Flowers, & Larkin, 2009). Data saturation was reached when no new themes emerged from adding additional participants (Morse, Barrett, Mayan, Olson, & Spiers, 2002; Silverman, 2010).

Before the interview began, interested participants were explained the research objectives of the interview, informed that participation was voluntary, and that they could interrupt and end the interview at any time. Participants did not receive any type of reward for study participation and were informed by the psychologist interviewers of their availability to discuss any issue related to their participation in the current study. All par- ticipants gave their written informed consent, none of them decided to leave the study during the interview, and none sought psychological counseling post interview. Ethical approval was obtained from the local Institutional Research Ethics Board.

Two trained psychologists interviewed participants in an individual interview session in a private room using a semistructured interview that focused on experiences with drugs and crime (examples of the interview questions are provided in the appendix at the end of this article). A

storytelling approach was used during the interview such that participants were invited to narrate particular events or situations drawn from their personal experience. The semistructured nature of this interview helped the psy- chologist interviewers to interact with inmate interview- ees in a dialogic process. This is fundamental in conducting any type of phenomenological study, as this type of research aims at offering participants the chance to express their deepest thoughts and feelings as freely as possible (Eatough & Smith, 2008). The prison did not allow audio recording of the interviews; therefore, while one psychol- ogist conducted the interview, the other wrote down the interviewee’s speech as accurately as possible and took field notes. The text was subsequently transcribed and reviewed by both psychologists performing the inter- views, and then discussed for final approval with the authors of this article.

Data Analysis

A data-driven approach (versus theory-driven) was used for text analysis. This data-driven approach is considered by many authors to be the most effective tool to investi- gate subjective experiences (Davidson, Ridgway, Kidd, Topor, & Borg, 2008; Neale, Allen, & Coombes, 2005). The method described by Lindseth and Norberg (2004) was used to perform the analyses and has been success- fully used with other qualitative studies on the experi- ences of health care patients (Benzein, Norberg, & Saveman, 2001; Ebbeskog & Ekman, 2001; Sundin, Norberg, & Jansson, 2001) and on opiate-dependents’ quality of life perceptions in a methadone treatment (De Maeyer et al., 2011).

Data analysis involved three methodological steps (Lindseth & Norberg, 2004): Naïve Reading, Thematic Structural Analysis, and Comprehensive Understanding. During the Naïve Reading step, the text was read twice to formulate a preliminary naïve understanding of each interview (first reading) and subsequently of the text as a whole (second reading). During the Thematic Structural Analysis step, the text was re-read and divided into mean- ing units (i.e., sentence parts, sentence, several sentences, and paragraphs that convey just one meaning) to identify dominant themes, subthemes, and relationships among themes and subthemes organizing the text, while during the Comprehensive Understanding step, the text was then interpreted as a whole. This analytic process was recur- sive and reflective because the themes/subthemes identi- fied in the second step were compared with the Naïve Understanding formulated in the first step, and consid- ered a first conjecture that was either validated or invali- dated by the subsequent structural analysis. Therefore, the Naïve Understanding acts as a guide for the subse- quent structural analysis.

Overall, the analytic process involved three phases. First, each author read the interviews and engaged in the three analytic steps (Phase 1). After completing Phase 1, the authors met to compare and discuss their insights (Phase 2). Because the analytic approach was inductive (i.e., data-driven versus theory-driven), the comparison with the research literature on drugs and crime, which was put “within brackets” for the text analysis, was con- ducted collectively by the authors only at the end of the whole process as a sort of “return to theory” (Phase 3).

Results

The text analyses identified six dominant themes that char- acterized the lived experience of drugs and crime of the interviewees. Those six themes are (a) growing up in a multi-problematic family of origin, (b) growing up in a devi- ant social context, (c) shifting from recreational to persistent drug use (not yet dependent drug use), (d) falling into depen- dent drug use, (e) being a drug-addicted offender, and (f) being incarcerated. Each theme involved a set of subthemes, which were considered further specifications of the meaning conveyed by the dominant themes and were obtained by identifying similarities and differences between interviews within themes. Dominant themes, subthemes, and additional exemplificative quotes are reported in Table 1.

Multi-Problematic Family of Origin

Participants’childhood was characterized as having multiple family problems. Drug and alcohol abuse by parents, broth- ers, or relatives was the most common family problem, fol- lowed by the presence of physical violence in the family:

My father used to take drugs in our apartment, locked in his bedroom . . . . My mother started drinking several years later to bear her relationship with my father.

(40-year-old participant)

Poverty, as well as crimes committed especially by par- ticipants’ fathers and brothers accompanied by subsequent incarcerations, loss of one or both parents, and HIV-related family member deaths were also mentioned. Few partici- pants described their childhood family life as healthy, “nor- mal,” contrary to drug and alcohol use and with a restrictive parental style. Overall, family relationships were character- ized by a significant lack of emotional engagement and mutual support, especially with the father and siblings.

Deviant Social Context

All participants, especially those who had multi-problematic families, grew up in neighborhoods where substance use and

crime such as drug dealing and thefts were considered nor- mal since preadolescence, or even rewarded with respect and admiration. Even when families were not abusing sub- stances and engaging in criminal activities, participants very often had older friends since early adolescence who did abuse substances and engage in criminal activities:

All my friends used to take drugs. . . . One by one they got arrested. I was a trouble maker and I used to hang out with older people who spent their time using drugs and committing crimes.

(32-year-old participant)

A few participants were “adopted” by organized crime families: These participants described their feelings of pride and omnipotence, as working for such families was considered an honor in their social context.

From Recreational to Persistent Drug Use

Older friends who were drug users and frequently drug dealers initiated nearly all participants to drug use. First drug experiences were typically in recreational contexts, especially discotheques and nightclubs and could not be described as regular because they were limited to particu- lar situations (e.g., parties):

We used to trip on Saturday nights. We divided it into four pieces, each of us took a quarter. It was good . . . . You trip on Saturday because you want to get high, you can dance more, feel the music at the disco. At the beginning you don’t do this during the week, you don’t want to be high on Mondays or Wednesdays.

(32-year-old participant)

Although this initial drug use was occasional, partici- pants told the interviewers that they were highly moti- vated to keep taking drugs due to the pleasure experienced, which can be described in the following terms: Feeling more adult and self-confident, feeling more social, and escaping from problems. Feeling more adult and self- confident refers to how many participants stated that using drugs made them feel as adult as the older friends who had initiated them to drug use with increased self- confidence (i.e., feeling nastier, feeling “the bad guy,” feeling courageous, feeling like “a lion”). Feeling more social refers to how many participants state that the first use of substances was initially limited to Saturday nights and involved “dance drugs” such as ecstasy and/or cocaine; this type of drug use was associated with posi- tive feelings (i.e., being more energetic, being more exhi- bitionist, being more talkative and funnier) and thus being more connected to people. Falling Into Dependent Drug Use

When talking about their shift from persistent use to dependent drug use, all participants identified a turning point characterized by the loss of control over the sub- stance and the onset of physical and psychological depen- dence. Three pathways toward dependence emerged from

the interviews. The first was characterized by a strong physical and psychological need for drugs to feel power- ful and limitless. Extreme, uncontrolled drug use was part of a “crazy party” lifestyle that provided participants with a feeling of grandiosity (i.e., being able to do/have every- thing, being constantly high). One may label this first pathway narcissistic. A strong physical dependence with the need for more and more drug was a consequence of this type of life:

For a long period I have been using cocaine all the time. I used to go to the disco every night, I got back very late, and I needed more cocaine to wake up in the morning and go to work. . . . I couldn’t stop. I felt omnipotent, like Superman, I didn’t need to sleep, I wasn’t hungry and I wasn’t even tired.

(34-year-old participant)

The second pathway may be defined as posttraumatic because it involved having experienced a trauma. Several participants radically increased drug use after a traumatic

event (e.g., the loss of a loved one, sudden death of their wife, separation from their partner, release after first incarceration, or a life-threatening car accident). Other traumas emerged from participants’ stories although not directly associated with the development of problematic drug use, such as the death from overdose of one or more friends, the diagnosis of a chronic disease, and the incur- able disease of a parent or a close relative. Overall, the drug use after these traumatic events was more character- ized by a significant loss of the self and aimed at self-destruction:

After the death of my wife I became a heavier and heavier drug user, it was like committing suicide.

(68-year-old participant)

The third pathway may be defined as pain relief and was characterized by psychological and emotional depen- dence on drugs as opposed to a physical dependence. The constant need for drugs by these participants was moti- vated by the need to be relieved from pain (i.e., worries, fear, anguish, physical pain in one case, which were not related to a specific traumatic event), to the point of becoming slaves to the substances. Several participants had been polyabusers (mainly cocaine, heroin, alcohol, and cannabis) and sometimes with injecting behaviors:

Heroin is comfort. . . . When you feel so much pain . . ., heroin helps you accept everything, you don’t care anymore.

(36-year-old participant)

Despite the distinctive pathways toward dependence and the different functions of the drugs associated with each pathway, all participants described the experience of falling into dependent drug use in very tragic terms. Expressions such as losing dignity, losing interest in life (e.g., sports, hobbies, studying, working), becoming self- ish (especially with significant others, such as partners and children), becoming liars, becoming violent (in gen- eral, but very frequently within intimate relationships), and feeling lonely were frequently used. Only a few of the participants sought treatment for their dependence.

Being a Drug-Addicted Offender

When talking about their experiences of drugs in associa- tion with crime, participants were not able to establish whether drug use or criminal activity came first as all of them were raised in social contexts where drugs and crime (especially drug dealing) occurred at the same time. However, different addiction and criminal careers emerged as a function of the pathways that led to substance depen- dence. Participants who followed the first pathway did not

establish a direct connection between drug use and crime, because both were fundamental components of living the high life (i.e., the so called la bella vita):

I loved the high life, having all the things I wanted. . . . I was a drug dealer for rich people so I was able to have motorcycles, cars, material things.

(41-year-old participant)

The majority of the participants stated that the main reason why they had initiated their criminal activity was to have more money for themselves and for their families (especially if they did not have a regular job), and not to sustain their drug use. The relationship between drugs and crime was more explicit and direct for those who were in the second and third pathways. These participants told the interviewers that crime was necessary for them to buy drugs:

There is a relationship between drug use and crime. Using drugs means keeping a sort of constant rhythm. You need money to buy it and for this reason you have to become a drug dealer, which may lead to incarceration.

(53-year-old participant)

Being Incarcerated

Participants highlighted two opposite aspects of their incarceration. Some of them focused more on the trau- matic dimensions involved by this experience, such as the separation from their family, especially from children, with negative consequences on their romantic relation- ships that led to a break up in several cases. One partici- pant experienced overcrowding in previous incarcerations that was described as an unbearable life condition. Other participants experienced incarceration as an opportunity for growth as men and fathers through the psychological treatment provided in prison. These opportunities involved accepting the past, expressing and elaborating the sense of guilt, identifying the psychological problems underlying drug use and crime, avoiding return to drugs and crimes after release, being good fathers, and being saved from extreme self-destructiveness:

Incarceration saved me otherwise I would have died from overdose or would have been shot by a cop during a robbery.

(37-year-old participant)

Discussion and Conclusion

This study explored the lived experience of drugs and crime of 25 drug-dependent male inmates interviewed in an Italian prison, providing an understanding of the

drug–crime relationship through the text analyses of their interviews based on a phenomenological method (Lindseth & Norberg, 2004). The six dominant themes showed that participant addiction and crime were char- acterized by common interrelated components: Nearly all participants had a disruptive childhood involving multi-problematic families and deviant social contexts where drugs and crime occurred at the same time and were considered normal or even rewarded. For this rea- son, the initiation to drugs and crime was described as a natural inevitable process, and we cannot conclude that drug use anticipated and caused crime, and vice versa. As highlighted by several authors (Hammersley, 2008, 2011; Maruna, 2008; Neale, 2000), having experienced bereavement, emotional, sexual, and physical abuse or neglect, violence and juvenile conflicts, having parents who abused drugs and alcohol, or family dysfunction in general may expose the individuals to the risk of devel- oping a drug dependence and criminal behaviors. All these potential sources of traumas are frequently present in the histories of drug-dependent people (Hammersley, 2011).

Another component of addiction with this sample regards their initial experiences with drugs, which involved recreational drug use for all participants and were associated with pleasure. The pleasant feelings of being more self-confident, social, limitless, and free from problems were powerful incentives to keep using drugs and ultimately led to a different type of drug use, which can be defined as persistent, but not yet as dependent. These findings provide support for the idea that the dichotomy established by the literature (Gilman, 1992), which contrasts recreational drug use—that is, part of a lifestyle aimed at seeking pleasure or leisure (Plant & Plant, 1992)—to dependent use, may be too simplistic. In this study, the third (intermediate) type of drug use that occurred during participants’ transition from recreational into dependent use was defined as persistent according to the definition provided by Simpson (2003):A more fre- quent and intense drug use, which may become a central daily activity, although not caused by dependence. The shift from recreational to persistent use was motivated by pleasure for all participants, yet the qualitative in-depth nature of this study identified three distinctive pathways leading to dependence characterized by a complete loss of control, as well as by the onset of physical and psycho- logical dependence.

For those participants who followed the first pathway, that is, the narcissistic pathway, drug use such as cocaine and amphetamines was linked to participants’ identity because it aimed at feeling constantly high, powerful, and grandiose to the point that the need for the drug became extremely intense and involved a strong physical depen- dence. For these participants, drugs and crime were not

directly associated: Both drug use and criminal behaviors allowed participants to feel limitless and to maintain a specific lifestyle (“la bella vita”), characterized by the desire to have everything. This type of addiction and criminal behavior―which is not represented in other research literature on drug use and crime―is congruent with the post-modern discourse that encourages consum- erism (the cult of objects) and narcissism (the constant need for self-affirmation) coupled with depression, anxi- ety, and the use of substances as identity crutches (Cigoli & Facchin, 2014).

The second pathway, that is, the posttraumatic path- way, provides support for a “trauma hypothesis” (Hammersley, 2011). According to this hypothesis, being subjected to stress or trauma and/or persisting effects of unresolved previous traumas might lead to periods of more intense drug use. This hypothesis frames the increased drug use as a dysfunctional coping strategy that can be used by the person to manage stress and trauma- related feelings. In the present work, the increased drug use after a trauma, which involved a loss of the self, was described as self-destructive and not as a tactic to avoid or numb negative feelings through intoxication. More properly, drug use to relieve pain characterized the third pathway, that is, pain relief pathway. These participants used any type of substance, including alcohol and heroin with injecting behaviors. Only the second and third path- ways involved a more direct connection between drugs and crime because the criminal activity was necessary to sustain drug use.

Consistently with our phenomenological perspective, which represents our epistemological position, the three pathways should not be considered explanatory theories of the transition from persistent into dependent drug use. In fact, they represent “patterns of meaning” emerging from an analytic process that moved from the analysis of each case to the examination of similarities and differ- ences across the 25 cases.

Another finding of this study regards the experience of incarceration, which has been considered by previous research to be another potential source of trauma because of unpleasant life conditions, conflicts with other con- victs, or even victimization, as well as the separation from family (Hammersley, 2011). Our findings suggest that when prisoners’ conditions are adequate and psycho- logical treatment is provided, imprisonment can be expe- rienced as an opportunity of personal growth through professional help, which only a few participants had sought before incarceration.

The findings presented in this article are limited for several reasons. First, its small sample, composed only of male participants, and the peculiarities of the Italian con- text are two obvious limitations. Moreover, the research was conducted in one single penitentiary, which is

particularly oriented toward the treatment as well as the social rehabilitation of detainees. The specific “culture” of this institution may have affected our findings, espe- cially with regard to the narration of the incarceration experience.

Second, although research tends to conceptualize the drug–crime connection as a pathway that almost inevitably leads to substance dependence, epidemiological data sug- gest that less than 2% of drug users become real depen- dent: The majority of people follow other pathways through drugs and crime (Hammersley, 2011). This study sample included a very small subgroup of dependent drug users who did not desist committing crime to the point of being incarcerated. Moreover, we did not investigate wom- en’s lived experience of drugs and crime, which may be significantly different as compared with that of men. Caution should therefore be reserved before definitive con- clusions, or theories can be drawn from our findings regarding the drug–crime link as well as the drug use itself.

This qualitative analysis may provide new lines for future quantitative studies on larger populations to test the generalizability of the three pathways identified and their specific associations with crime. However, qualitative and quantitative studies of addiction should be considered complementary (Neale et al., 2005). Future research should also compare those individuals who develop dependent drug use with those who do not become depen- dent or even desist from drug use and crime. As suggested by Simpson (2003), a career-based approach, which involves a lifelong perspective focused on trajectories, changes, and transitions, may be particularly useful to acknowledge the complex nature of the drug–crime link.
